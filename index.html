Interview prep
Three questions asked at every anesthesia interview. Practice out loud until natural -- not rehearsed.
Q1 -- "Why didn't you match anesthesia the first time?"
"My application had real strengths -- I interviewed at UCSD, Cedars-Sinai, OHSU, and UC Davis. But I had not yet demonstrated sustained clinical performance in a rigorous environment over time. I chose IM specifically to provide exactly that evidence. My performance in IM is my answer to that question."

Key: honest, forward-looking, names the gap and closes it immediately.
Q2 -- "Why anesthesia -- and why now?"
"Internal medicine gave me a longitudinal view of how chronic disease evolves. That training made me increasingly drawn to the perioperative window -- where those chronic trajectories become acutely consequential. Anesthesia requires real-time simultaneous management of interacting organ systems under time pressure. That is the cognitive environment where I feel most engaged."

Key: no vigilance, no advocacy, no last line of defense. Anesthesia as logical destination of IM reasoning.
Q3 -- "What do you bring that others don't?"
"Three things. First, perioperative AKI research -- I am lead investigator on a study examining surgical AKI risk factors. I understand perioperative physiology at a research level. Second, IM-trained longitudinal physiologic reasoning -- most applicants have not managed the chronic disease progression that creates perioperative risk. I have. Third, operational leadership at scale -- $140K fundraising infrastructure, workflow redesign for 300+ families -- which maps directly to perioperative systems thinking."

cms documentation rules — internalized
What makes MDM "high complexity" for billing?
Must hit 2 of 3: (1) Multiple chronic conditions OR acute illness threatening life/organ — document "acute on chronic HFrEF with hemodynamic instability." (2) Extensive data — review/order labs, imaging, AND independent interpretation of external records or independent historian. (3) High risk management — drug requiring intensive monitoring, hospitalization, or decision not to resuscitate.
How do you document "independent interpretation" of a test?
Don't write "CXR read by radiology." Write: "CXR reviewed independently: bilateral interstitial edema, cardiomegaly, no pleural effusion greater than trace — consistent with acute decompensation." Your words, your finding, your clinical correlation.
A&P phrase bank — CMS-safe language
Risk stratification: "Patient meets high-risk criteria given [X] — warranting inpatient management for monitoring and titration."  |  Decision complexity: "Multiple competing diagnoses considered including [DDx] — clinical picture most consistent with [dx] given [reason]."  |  Escalation reasoning: "Clinical trajectory warrants escalation — patient not responding to initial therapy as evidenced by [objective data]."
One-liner formula that impresses attendings
[Age][sex] with [key PMH] presenting with [chief complaint] in the context of [precipitant], most likely [leading dx], with [complication/concern] requiring [key action]. Example: "67M with ischemic cardiomyopathy EF 30% presenting with 2-week dyspnea on exertion and 8lb weight gain, most likely acute decompensated HFrEF in the setting of dietary indiscretion and medication non-adherence, now requiring IV diuresis with close hemodynamic monitoring."
Physical exam — present what's relevant, not what's normal
Bad: "Heart RRR, lungs CTA bilaterally." Good: "Cardiovascular: RRR, 2/6 holosystolic murmur at apex radiating to axilla — unchanged from prior. Pulmonary: bibasilar crackles to mid-lung fields bilaterally, dullness to percussion at bases. Extremities: 2+ pitting edema to knees, no calf tenderness." Findings anchor your reasoning. Normal findings can support your DDx — say why they matter.

🚨 Critical fix: Leech threshold = 1 → Change to 8
A leech threshold of 1 means any card you miss once gets tagged as a leech. Everything becomes a leech. Anki's default is 8 (wrong 4 times = leech). Set yours to 8 — it flags genuinely hard cards, not normal learning difficulty. This is your most urgent settings fix.
⚠ Auto Advance: 4s question timer → Disable or set 20s+
For Step 3 and anesthesia boards, clinical vignettes need 30–90 seconds of real thinking. A 4-second auto-advance is training your brain to guess reflexively, not reason. Disable Speed Focus Mode during active study. Use it only for quick review runs on cards you know cold.
⚠ Learning steps: 1m 25m → Change to 1m 10m 1440m
Adding a 1-day step (1440m) forces the card into the next day before it graduates. This dramatically improves consolidation — you test recall after a sleep cycle, not just 25 minutes later. This one change measurably improves long-term retention with minimal extra reviews.

When to reoptimize FSRS parameters
Every 2–3 months: open Anki → your deck → Options → FSRS → Optimize. Let it recalculate from your actual review history. Do this after any major change in study intensity (starting residency, coming off a hard rotation). Your parameters will drift as you build more data — optimizing keeps your intervals accurate.
Six months before Step 3: change retention to 0.92
Bump desired retention from 0.90 to 0.92 approximately 6 months before your Step 3 date. This tightens review intervals and increases daily burden by ~20%, but you will retain more. Do this gradually — 0.90 → 0.91 → 0.92 over 2 months so Anki can reschedule without flooding you.

procedure reference library
★ VASCULAR ACCESS
Peripheral IV ★
Every rotation — do 5 before asking nurses
Use largest vein in AC. 18g for resuscitation. Confirm blood flashback → advance → remove stylet. Flush with 10mL NS.
Arterial Line – Radial ★★
ICU, Day Admitting — ask to place every one you can
Modified Allen's test first. 20g catheter-over-needle. Palpate radial between tendons. 30-45° angle. Flash → flatten → advance. Constant BP monitoring intraoperatively = anesthesia daily use.
Central Venous Catheter – IJ/Subclavian ★★
ICU rotations (x4 blocks this year) — your primary central line rotation
US guidance mandatory. Seldinger technique: needle → flash → guidewire → dilate → catheter. Confirm placement with CXR before use. Subclavian = lowest infection risk. IJ = fastest. Ask to do both.
★★ AIRWAY
Endotracheal Intubation – RSI ★★★
ICU, ED, Night Float — ask to intubate every patient in the ICU
RSI sequence: Preoxygenate (BNR if time) → Etomidate 0.3mg/kg or Ketamine 1-2mg/kg → Succinylcholine 1.5mg/kg or Rocuronium 1.2mg/kg → Laryngoscope → confirm with waveform capnography. This is what anesthesiologists do 10× per day. Know it in your sleep.
Bag-Mask Ventilation ★★★
Before every intubation — the skill that saves lives when intubation fails
Two-hand CE grip preferred. Jaw thrust — not just chin lift. Adequate seal before pushing air. Anesthesia pearl: if you can't mask ventilate, you can't safely intubate. The most undervalued skill in all of medicine.
PLEURAL / ABDOMINAL
Thoracentesis
Wards, ICU — ask to do every effusion workup
US guidance. Posterior approach, 1-2 intercostal spaces below fluid level. Superior rib margin (avoid NVB). Light's criteria on all pleural fluid. Diagnostic tap = 50mL enough.
Paracentesis
Wards, Admitting — cirrhosis is common at Kaiser SF
Left lower quadrant preferred (avoid epigastric vessels). US mark. SAAG on all first taps. Large volume: 6g albumin per liter removed ≥5L. Peritoneal fluid cell count, culture, glucose, LDH, protein.
Lumbar Puncture
Wards, Night Float — meningitis/SAH workup
L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace. Lateral decubitus or sitting. Bevel up, aim for umbilicus. Measure opening pressure always. Tubes 1-4: cell count, glucose/protein, culture, extras. Clear fluid = don't celebrate yet (SAH can be xanthochromic).
OTHER BEDSIDE
ABG – Arterial Blood Gas
ICU, Night Float, Admitting — do every one you can
Radial preferred. Palpate pulse, 45° angle, advance until flash, then back off 2mm. Hold 5 min pressure. Interpret: pH → PaCO2 → compensation → PaO2 → A-a gradient → lactate.
